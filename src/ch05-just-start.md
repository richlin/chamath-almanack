# Just Start

*On compound interest, investing in what you love, and showing up 15 years later.*

---

Chamath's investing philosophy begins with the simplest possible advice: start. Don't wait for the right amount. Don't ask for permission. Don't tell anyone.

---

> I think one of the most important things in investing is to just start. It's kind of like making a baby. You just got to start. Talking about it doesn't actually help you make a baby.

---

He's blunt about what stops most people.

---

> The thing about starting is there is this incredible trick that people will play on you which is the only people that care how much you start with is other people. What they want to do is they want to feel better about their decisions.
>
> So, I would encourage you to just start and just not tell anybody and then show up 10 or 15 years later with a huge war chest and shove it up their ass.

---

## The Compound Interest Table

The first practical tool he recommends is almost embarrassingly simple.

---

> If you're going to start investing, the first rule is I would manually build my own compound interest table.
>
> I'll tell you a story about an incredibly famous athlete. He signed a ginormous contract and I built a Google sheet, took this new salary, took out the taxes, what you have to pay for the manager and the agent. And I showed him a compound interest table and his mind was blown. And I said, "Let's assume it was 1/1,000th of this." And his mind was still blown. And I said, "That is the power of compound interest."
>
> When you start small, you can post huge numbers if you focus on a systematic process and these things can grow over 20 and 30 years. And you just have to be okay with that.

---

## Invest in What You Know

His advice to first-time investors — including his own children — is to start with what's already in front of them.

---

> What do you love? What do you think is good and useful in your life? Let's go find if that company is public. Buy that thing.

---

He tells the story of his two sons, both given a couple hundred dollars from a golf bet on vacation.

---

> My older son said, "Nah. But I like the price action of this Virgin Galactic thing." Plows 200 in, makes like 3,000. Sells it, buys a computer. He's never to be seen again in the stock market.
>
> My younger son, however, says, "Well, I like Xbox, I like PlayStation, and I like Nintendo." And he bought all three and he's been a steady compounder — he made like a 30% compounded return the last 3 years.

---

Two kids, two approaches. One speculated and left. One invested in what he knew and stayed. The lesson writes itself.
